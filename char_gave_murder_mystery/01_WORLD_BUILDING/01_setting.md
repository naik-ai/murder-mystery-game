## चार गावे - World Building

## TIME PERIOD

**Year:** 2002-2003
**Season:** Transition from severe drought (2001-2002) to massive monsoon (2002-2003)

### Why This Matters:
- No mobile internet (isolation)
- Frequent power cuts ("load shedding")
- Heavy reliance on folklore to explain the unknown
- Economic desperation from drought years
- Sudden environmental shift creates instability

---

## LOCATION

**Region:** Rural Maharashtra, India
**Specifics:** A cluster of four villages, semi-rural, surrounded by scrubland and sparse forest
**Nearest Town:** 2+ hours by bus
**Police Station:** One constable posted locally, serious crimes go to taluka level

---

## THE FOUR VILLAGES

### वाडा (Wada) - The Manor
- **Meaning:** Large house/estate
- **Character:** Old zamindari (landlord) area, declining
- **Population:** ~120
- **Key Feature:** The zamindar's crumbling mansion
- **Atmosphere:** Faded grandeur, secrets, old money guilt

### बाभूळ (Babhul) - Acacia Grove  
- **Meaning:** Named after Babul/Acacia trees
- **Character:** Scrubland edge village, traditional
- **Population:** ~200
- **Key Feature:** Access to talbed routes, old survey office nearby
- **Atmosphere:** Watchful, people know land-record secrets

### तळेगाव (Talegaon) - Lake Village
- **Meaning:** Village by the lake
- **Character:** Central village, most populated
- **Population:** ~350
- **Key Feature:** Closest to Korde Tal (dry lake), administrative center
- **Atmosphere:** Fear central here, lake is visible daily

### वस्ती (Vasti) - The Settlement
- **Meaning:** Settlement/hamlet (often lower caste)
- **Character:** Labor settlement, poorest
- **Population:** ~180
- **Key Feature:** Workers for other villages, invisible witnesses
- **Atmosphere:** Knows everything, says nothing, survival mode

---

## KEY LOCATIONS

### कोरडे तळे (Korde Tal) - Dry Lake
- Central geographic feature
- Dried up during drought, fills unpredictably during monsoon
- Site of all child drownings
- Villagers avoid it, especially at dusk
- Ash and medical waste residue have contaminated it

### भट्टी (Bhatti) - The Kiln Area
- Cluster of brick kilns on outskirts
- ~150 workers and families
- Burns "dirty" fuel (damp wood, unauthorized waste)
- Creates permanent yellow-grey smoke layer
- Economic heart of the region

### राखे (Rakhe) - The Ashes
- Official ash dumping ground
- Near kiln area
- What villagers think is the only dumping site
- Actually, illegal burning/disposal happens at the Tal

### धुके (Dhuke) - The Mist
- Low-lying area, perpetually foggy during monsoon
- Where Aarav was found after escaping
- Eerie, avoided by villagers
- Natural phenomenon used to reinforce ghost beliefs

### देवस्थान (Devasthan) - The Temple
- Ancient temple, pre-dates villages
- On elevated ground between Wada and Babhul
- Overlooks dry lake
- Center of religious activity, especially during fear periods

### दवाखाना (Davakhana) - The Dispensary
- Small government Primary Health Centre (PHC)
- At crossroads near Talegaon
- Accessible to all villages
- Where Leena works
- Where Arjun comes for medical records

## ATMOSPHERE ELEMENTS

### The Smoke
- Kiln smoke trapped by monsoon air pressure
- Yellow-grey haze over valley
- Causes headaches, confusion, fatigue
- Villagers attribute symptoms to curse
- Actually mild CO poisoning

### The Mold
- Monsoon humidity (95%+) triggers mold bloom
- Black mold (Stachybotrys) in wooden houses
- Traditional homes built with unsealed teak
- Walls "bleed" black
- Spores cause brain fog, visual glitches

### The Water
- Kiln ash contains lye, heavy metals
- Washed into water sources during monsoon
- Causes stomach illness, irritability
- Lake water particularly contaminated
- Villagers drink from wells, but runoff affects some

### The Darkness
- Frequent power cuts
- No street lights
- Kerosene lamps, candles
- Night is genuinely dark
- Easy to see "shapes" in shadows

### The Sound
- Monsoon rain on tin roofs (deafening)
- Kiln burning (constant low roar)
- Frogs, insects (overwhelming at night)
- Hard to hear someone approaching
- Easy to imagine whispers

## DAILY LIFE (2002-2003)

### Communication
- No mobile phones (rare, expensive, no signal)
- One STD/PCO booth in Talegaon
- Letters via postmaster
- News travels by word of mouth
- Radio for news, but unreliable power

### Transportation
- Bullock carts common
- Few motorcycles (status symbol)
- One bus to town, twice daily
- Walking between villages (30-60 min)
- Roads become mud during monsoon

### Economy
- Agriculture (struggling after drought)
- Kiln work (primary employer)
- Forest labor (legal and illegal)
- Domestic service (Vasti to Wada)
- Small shops in Talegaon

### Social Structure
- Caste still strongly observed
- Zamindar family respected but declining
- Kiln owners rising in power
- Vasti residents treated as invisible
- Women's movement restricted

---

## THE DROUGHT YEARS (Background)

### What Happened (2001-2002):
- Worst drought in decades
- Crops failed completely
- Livestock died
- Farmer suicides increased
- Mass migration to cities
- Those who stayed accumulated debt

### Psychological Impact:
- Collective trauma
- Fatalism increased
- Religious activity increased
- "Curse" explanations more acceptable
- Trust in institutions decreased
- Desperation made people vulnerable

### Economic Impact:
- Land changed hands (desperate sales)
- Kiln families gained power (kept operating)
- Zamindar sold more land
- Debt relationships formed
- Power structures shifted

---

## THE MONSOON (Current)

### What's Happening (2002-2003):
- Rain returned, but extreme
- Flooding in low areas
- Lake filling unpredictably
- Mold blooming everywhere
- Disease spreading
- But also: hope returning

### Why It Matters for Story:
- Environmental symptoms at peak
- Lake where children died is active again
- Old fears resurface
- Economic pressure to "move on" vs need to grieve
- New deaths (Timeline 3) during this period

## SUPERSTITION CONTEXT

### What Villagers Believe:
- The lake is cursed
- Twin girls' spirits live there
- They take children (always in pairs)
- Siblings are especially vulnerable
- Certain times are dangerous (dusk, monsoon, new moon)
- Rituals can provide protection (but clearly failing)

### Why They Believe:
- Three incidents over 15 years (pattern)
- Zamindar himself "saw" the ghosts
- No other explanation makes sense to them
- Authority figures (priest, sarpanch) reinforce belief
- Questioning the curse feels like inviting it

### How Belief Functions:
- Keeps people away from lake (convenient for killers)
- Prevents real investigation
- Provides psychological closure (fate, not failure)
- Creates community bonding through shared fear
- Makes victims' families part of larger narrative (not alone)

## SENSORY DETAILS FOR WRITING

### Sight:
- Yellow-grey haze over everything
- Black mold creeping up walls
- Lake water murky, sometimes foaming
- Kerosene lamp shadows
- Monsoon grey sky, rarely seeing sun

### Sound:
- Rain drumming (constant during monsoon)
- Kiln roar (background always)
- Frogs at night (deafening)
- Whispered conversations (fear of being heard)
- Temple bells (increased frequency)

### Smell:
- Kiln smoke (sulfurous, acrid)
- Mold (damp, rotting)
- Wet earth (petrichor, but also decay)
- Incense from temple (trying to purify)
- Cooking fires (familiar, comforting)

### Touch:
- Humidity (everything damp)
- Mud (unavoidable during monsoon)
- Rough cloth (rural clothing)
- Sticky surfaces (mold, moisture)
- Heat from kilns (even in rain)

### Taste:
- Water tastes metallic (contamination)
- Food tastes off (mold in storage)
- Chai is refuge (one thing that's right)
- Dust in dry season (remembered)
- Salt of sweat (constant in humidity)
